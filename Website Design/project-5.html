Project Overview

My project will evaluate how literary hypertext can be used as an avenue for women and nonbinary individuals with hyperandrogenism to write illness narratives. Literary hypertext is a form of digital story writing that calls on the reader to participate in the narrative’s unfolding by selecting hyperlink options which branch the narrative into nonlinear directions. There has been a revival of scholarly and public interest in literary hypertext in the past 5 years, owing to the genre’s steep rise in popularity amongst minority game designers (Anthropy 21). Concentrated in the discipline of Game Studies, there is limited scholarly research on the potential for literary hypertext as a tool for writing therapeutic illness narratives.

Turning towards the intersection of Literature, Health and Digital Humanities, my project questions how women and nonbinary individuals with hyperandrogenism can use hypertext technology to write illness narratives that construct positive relationships between their identities and the world. I created an online story-writing module and hypertext tutorial for ten participants with hyperandrogenism who have written and submitted their own stories based on their illness experience. This research will contribute to the concrete realization of a novel pathway to inform therapeutic approaches for emotional well-being related to gendered illness.

In The Wounded Storyteller, Arthur Frank wrote that major illness has the potential to disrupt the planned destination of our life, but the practice of illness narrative – autobiographical accounts of the experience of illness – reclaims the capacity for telling our story (53). Despite the therapeutic value of traditional illness narratives, the medium has been criticized for upholding barriers to entry for marginalized groups (Couser, Signifying Bodies 58). Some hypertext tools have cultivated a movement of support from marginalized game developers who are often excluded from masculinist, white spaces of game development (Anthropy 90; Harvey 96).

Literary hypertext is a culture of free production and distribution (tools are often free to download, open-source, and can export stories as HTML files to be published online) and is easy to use as no coding knowledge is required by the author. Beyond technological and financial accessibility, cyberliterature subverts assumed norms in traditional narrative mediums by promoting experimentation through multiple endings, branching plot, and interactive components (Harvey 101). For illness narratives, branching hypertext has potential as a productive platform for authors to learn how power functions in their story as they build in narrative choices that yield consequences (Thevenin 1). Literary hypertext has been taken up in Game Studies as a platform that blows open access to game design for marginalized communities, and I began to wonder how this access could translate for women with hyperandrogenism to write and publish illness narratives.

Hyperandrogenism, a condition of “excessive” levels of testosterone in the female body, can cause “masculinizing” symptoms including excess body and facial hair, male-pattern baldness, infertility, increased muscle mass and absence of menstrual bleeding (Bazarganipour et al. 371). The disease affects 5-10% of reproductive-aged women (Yildiz 167). Medical literature affirms that the aesthetic expressions of hyperandrogenism can be benign (Sonksen et al. 826), however, in clinical practice women diagnosed with hyperandrogenism are prescribed medication to lower testosterone or else advised of cosmetic “solutions” including laser hair removal, medication for male-pattern hair loss, and bleaching cream to “restore” feminine embodiment (Cannon 15). The condition has been employed as a justification to call into question which bodily signifiers and hormonal nuances quantify biological sex. Through the reinforcement of sex norms, women and individuals with hyperandrogenism are made to feel that they are not “feminine” enough.

Due to experiences of perceived subjugation in the medical encounter some women and individuals are turning to online illness narratives to write their abject bodies (Butler, Bodies that Matter 3) into a budding corporeal politic. My MA research examined the writing in an online women’s health forum and explored the discourse on gender and illness that was developing in this space. I found that multiple, deviating dialogs were emerging: the defiance of the medical system cultivated in this online space was conditional on a collective agreement to work to combat or “cure” symptoms that challenged standard femininity. Indeed, there are serious implications for individuals when the sexed body is read “incorrectly” or when it is unreadable at all (see Butler, Undoing Gender). Performing gender “appropriately” for these women therefore becomes a mode of preservation. I found that these blog posts behaved as illness narratives in the ways they worked to destabilize medicalized regulatory discourse (Couser, Recovering Bodies 34).

Turning to scholars who have shaped methods of Digital Discourse Analysis (Thurlow) and Critical Narrative Analysis (Souto-Manning), I have developed a new Critical Discourse Analysis for Digital-born Narrative. New interpretations of CDA acknowledge a distinct lack of tools to examine the sociolinguistic and discursive phenomena of personal narratives in new media. My research will analyze interactive language practice in literary hypertext by attending to issues of meaning-making through narrative themes, larger discourses of power, and digital components (use of interactivity, hypertext, images, or sound).

Twine, one platform for writing literary hypertext, is a freely accessible, open-source tool for multilinear narratives. It was developed by interactive fiction author Chris Klimas in 2009 to reduce barriers to entry for game creators of marginalized identities. My project utilizes Twine as the storytelling engine does not require the user to have previous coding knowledge (Skains 10). Users can access the program through their personal browsers, build interactive story structures, and publish their writing directly to HTML.

I have recruited ten women-identifying and nonbinary participants, as this group size is narrow enough to build an individual relationship between the researcher and the participant, a component of my feminist research design. Participants meet the eligibility criteria (are at least 18 years-old, are English-speakers, and have experienced at least two symptoms of hyperandrogenism). Potential participants were invited to a screening interview that documented eligibility and diversity markers. Eligible participants are completing a two-hour module accessed privately in an internet browser involving storyboarding, a Twine tutorial, and a guide for building a multilinear illness narrative. Once stories were completed, participants were interviewed about their experiences using literary hypertext to write an illness narrative and invited to share their stories in a secure folder for fellow participants to “play”. This project has REB clearance and follows all protocols suggested by the review board.
